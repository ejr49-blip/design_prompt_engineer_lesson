## Prompt 1

- **Phase:** Exploration / Production / Critique *(pick one)*
- **Prompt name:** *(e.g., "Concept Map", "Draft Labels", "Expert Critique")*
- **What I sent:**

```
Provide an overview of the Bauhaus movement with a focus on graphic design.
Include founding year, philosophy, visual characteristics, and its influence on modern design.
Avoid speculation and flag any uncertainty.
```

- **What I got back (summary):**

The Bauhaus was founded in 1919 in Weimar, Germany by architect Walter Gropius.
It promoted the integration of art, craft, and technology, emphasizing function over ornament.
In graphic design, Bauhaus favored geometric forms, asymmetrical layouts, and sans-serif typography.
The movement influenced modernist design by promoting clarity, rational structure, and mass production principles.
Some aspects of Bauhaus graphic output were less standardized than later Swiss Style work.

## Prompt 2

- **Phase 2**
- **Initial Exploration (Swiss Style)**
- ****

```
Summarize Swiss Style (International Typographic Style) with attention to typography, layout systems, and design philosophy.
Include time period and notable characteristics.
Do not invent designers if uncertain.
```

- Swiss Style emerged in Switzerland during the 1950s and became influential internationally.
It emphasized clarity, objectivity, and readability through the use of grid systems.
Designers favored sans-serif typefaces, asymmetrical layouts, and neutral visual language.
The movement sought to remove personal expression in favor of universal communication.
Swiss Style became foundational for corporate and institutional graphic design.

```
Explain New Wave Typography as a reaction to Swiss Style.
Focus on how and why designers rejected Swiss principles.
Include general time period and visual traits.
```

- **What I got back (summary):**
New Wave Typography emerged in the late 1970s and 1980s as a reaction to the rigidity of Swiss Style.
Designers rejected strict grid systems and objective neutrality.
Typography became expressive, layered, and experimental.
Legibility was sometimes intentionally challenged in favor of visual impact.
The movement aligned with postmodern design thinking.
